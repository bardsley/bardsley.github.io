International Student Support over Brexit

What needs to change

At the moment, international student support within UHI is weak and sporadic, especially in view of Brexit. Within UHI, contacting international student support staff, whether this is regional or local, creates significant challenges, and as a consequence of this, clear, coherent information is difficult to access.

International students are sometimes entrusted to their PATs, but this does not always satisfy the necessity for further support or information. At times it seems that international students are left to themselves, lacking a solid underlying support system.

What can HISA do about it

HISA can campaign to increase international student support awareness on a regional and national level, working in partnership with other SAs. HISA can lobby UHI to establish specific international support staff in each AP, easy to contact wherever in need.

HISA can campaign for the increase of international student support, facilitating the flow of information about entry requirements, immigration scheme, student loans, and much more.

HISA can invest in the creation of an international student package, containing useful information, signposting websites and staff.

Facts behind the need for change

This policy relies on a solid base of personal experience as an international student within UHI for the past two years.

Throughout this period, I have come across several situations in which international student information was difficult to access, and staff could not point me in the right direction. This went from the application process, during which local staff omitted essential information about the translation of documents, to the day-to-day life, when sending queries to international@uhi.ac.uk.

A few international students met throughout the academic journey spoke of similar experiences. The need for increased student support over such challenging times was made clear by the last NUS student survey. Although this focussed on the impact of the pandemic on students’ mental health, I believe it still goes to highlight how vulnerable the student body is, and how important it is for it to be properly supported.

International students can be considered particularly vulnerable during times of profound social and political change, and as a student association, HISA should take a stand.

Chapliancy

What needs to change

UHI and the academic partners have overlooked an important element of the student welfare ever since UHI was formed. UHI is the only university in Scotland which has no chaplaincy provision on offer to support the students.

Chaplaincy service plays a crucial part in the infrastructure designed to support all students regardless of their background. This provision is for all students from Christians, Muslims, LGBTQ+, or any other belief or identity. Chaplaincy service protects students welfare, supports students in crisis, gives advice on equality to the university, and facilitates students' needs at the university.

Students are under immense pressure due to COVID restrictions and the impact of this period will continue to add significant pressure for our students. This is why we need a chaplaincy service to be established as soon as possible.

What can HISA do about it

HISA is the voice of students at UHI and as such is the only one that can lobby for a post creation for a chaplaincy service. This is why I ask the HISA CON to vote for a crucial new provision to be provided for the students at UHI.

If successful the motion would require to be lead by the HISA President, with the support of key student officers from the HISA Executive. The reason for a high-level push would be required to get UHI to assign funds for a new service creation under these uncertain economic times.

Facts behind the need for change

  • Chaplains main activities at a university: 1. Pastoral support/counselling, 2. Building community, 3. Administration, 4. Pastoral support/counselling for staff.
    (Source; Understanding Chaplaincy in UK Universities, Trust, Peace and Social Relations Research Centre, Durham University)
  • On the average UK universities have 10.4 chaplains, of which 3.4 are paid (wonkhe.com).
  • For example, Glasgow University has a Univesity Chaplain and Chaplaincy Secretary. And volunteer chaplains for Anglican/Episcopalian, Church of Scotland Chaplain, Hindu Chaplain, Humanist Chaplain, Jewish Chaplain, LGBTQ+ Chaplain, Muslim Chaplain, Roman Catholic Chaplain, Sikh Chaplain, URC Chaplain, and three chapel music staff. (Source; gla.ac.uk)
  • UHI is the only Scottish university without a chaplaincy provision. (Research was undertaken personally via online sources).

HISA will aim to Increase Opportunities for Students to Engage with their Local Communities and the Charitable Organisations Within

What needs to change

Interaction between students and the local community is often sparse and at some points can be negative in nature. For Students moving to term time accommodation, integrating into local communities can be difficult.

By reaching out to more community and charitable organisations we will be able to foster closer relationships between students and “locals” and fight loneliness and isolation within both groups.

What can HISA do about it

HISA should aim to integrate community outreach into day to day working and should look at appointing community ambassadors to work specifically towards improving community links.

HISA should continually signpost students to local support networks.

Class reps should be continually updated of progress.

Facts behind the need for change

  • 52 per cent of students say their mental health has deteriorated or been affected negatively by Covid-19.
  • Only 20 per cent of students have sought mental health support.
  • 29% of those who said their mental health had been negatively affected had sought help.
  • Of those seeking help 57% are satisfied with how they’ve been supported.

-NUS Student Survey

SAD Lamps

What needs to change

Currently we are in a pandemic but usually our students will spend huge amounts of time inside during the short daylight hours of Winter days, and for our full time students many of these sunlight hours will be spent inside at college.

What can HISA do about it

HISA can lobby UHI to put SAD lamps in all learning centers for use from students and staff to help reduce melatonin production during the Winter months when sunlight is scarce and weak.

Facts behind the need for change

SAD lamps are thought to reduce melatonin levels in the brain as the light hits the retina. This helps boost brain and mental health and reduce the affects of Seasonal Affective Disorder.

Hidden Student Fees

What needs to change

Students on especially specialist courses are often facing additional costs during their studies for often expensive equipment, IT software and residencies. These costs should be made openly known to the students prior to their studies.

What can HISA do about it

HISA should lobby UHI to make the costs publicly available on their course information websites to enable students to be aware of any of the costs prior to their studies.

Facts behind the need for change

The access to additional equipment, IT software and residencies is linked to the student’s success on their course.

Students are often employed in low-income sectors. Additional unknown costs could create unnecessary stress and hardship for our students.

Regionally Linked up FE Curriculum

What needs to change

We see huge differences in the student experience in especially further education courses across UHI and its academic partners, while the HE experience is more equal and less dependent on the geographical area of the student.

While UHI's Higher education curriculum is linked up and quality monitored regionally, we currently don't see this in Further education courses. A regionally linked up further education curriculum would enable students to have equity in student experience, more good practice sharing in teaching methods and there would be less duplication of work.

What can HISA do about it

HISA should work in partnership with UHI and the academic partners to regionally link up FE curriculum where possible within the ongoing curriculum review.

Facts behind the need for change

UHI is currently undergoing a curriculum review that is looking at linked up FE curriculum.

The Scottish funding council is currently undergoing a funding review for Further and Higher education across Scotland. Their findings of phase 1 suggests a more tertiary approach to funding.
